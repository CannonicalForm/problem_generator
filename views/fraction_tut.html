Fractions

Without thinking, and on a near daily basis, people use fractions to take stock of much around their lives : "There's still half a gallon of milk, so we don't need to go to the store," or "There's only a third of a tank of gas left." Yet, when pressed in an academic setting to perform even simple calculations with fractions, such as \[\frac{5}{9} + \frac{2}{3} \left(= \frac{11}{9}\right)\] the numbers can turn on us, and what was second nature before can become alien. This page is designed to help alleviate that feeling, and with appropriate practice, allow all such calculations involving fractions to be done with ease.

What is a Fraction?

This might seem so obvious that it hurts. After all, thinking up examples of fractions is trivial. However, consider the following list of symbols: which are valid fractions?

  • \(\frac{1}{3}\)
  • \(\frac{3.2}{6}\)
  • \(\frac{-9}{8}\)
  • \(\frac{7}{0}\)
  • \(1\frac{3}{7}\)

If the second, fourth, or fifth examples appear to be fractions, the need for a common vocabulary to describe these objects is apparent.

Properly speaking, a fraction is the ratio of two integers (numbers like 1, 2, -4, 7, ...) a and b, where \(b \ne 0\) and is written as a/b. The top of fraction a is called the numerator, while the bottom of the fraction b is called the denominator.

Using the previous definition, we may determine why \(\frac{3.2}{6}\), \(\frac{7}{0}\) and \(1\frac{3}{7}\) are not fractions. For the first, note that the definition requires that both the numerator and the denominator must be integers. However, 3.2 is not an integer, as it is a decimal number, so \(\frac{3.2}{6}\) is not a fraction. In the second example, note that the denominator is explicitly prohibitted from being 0. Therefore \(\frac{7}{0}\) cannot be a fraction. This explicit prohibition is to prevent division by zero, which can play some pretty terrible tricks in mathematics. The last example is most commonly refered to as a mixed number. It is an integer attached to a fractional number, which corresponds to the fraction \(\frac{11}{8}\). However, it is not itself a fraction, as addition, multiplication and reduction work slightly differently with mixed numbers than with fractions.

Lowest Common Form

Typically a fraction is presented in its lowest common form, or as already reduced. A fraction being already reduced is the difference between \(\frac{5}{10}\) and \(\frac{1}{2}\): the second is equivilent to the first, but is presented in its lowest common form. While the lowest common form of fractions can be relatively straightforward, it is not without it's difficulties. Consider the following fractions: which one is reduced?

  • a. \(\frac{5}{10}\)
  • b. \(\frac{15}{28}\)
  • c. \(\frac{12}{33}\)
  • d. \(\frac{35}{42}\)
  • e. \(\frac{8}{10}\)

With the exception of a and e, it can be rather challenging to determine which fractions are given in reduced form. Take the following definition,

A fraction \(\frac{a}{b}\) is not reduced if a and b share a common divisor.

Using this notation, we find that in (d) 7 divides both 35 (\(7*5 = 35 \)), 42 (\(7*6 = 42\)), so that (d) is not reduced. Similarly 3 divides both 12 and 33, which shows that(c) is not reduced. Now, try as we might, we cannot find a common divisor for 15 and 28, which would seem to indicate that(b) is in lowest common form. Indeed, since the only divisors of 15 are 3 and 5, and the divisors of 28 are 2,4,7,14, there are no common divisors, so (b) is reduced.
